ANNOUNCEMENT FROM THE OFFICE OF THE GOVERNOR OF CALIFORNIA

Date: June 8, 2025
Contact: Office of the Governor, Communications Division

Outline for the New California Housing Authority (nCHA)

A Bold Institutional Commitment to Solve California’s Interlinked Housing, Infrastructure, Energy, and Human Development Crises

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

California today stands at a historic crossroads. While the state boasts the world’s fifth-largest economy and leads in technological innovation, it simultaneously faces some of the most acute challenges in housing affordability, infrastructure development, homelessness, environmental degradation, and income inequality.

The formation of the New California Housing Authority (nCHA) is a direct and ambitious response to these intersecting crises. Drawing inspiration from the historical example of the Hong Kong Housing Authority, which after the devastating Shek Kip Mei fire in 1953 provided safe housing for over 200,000 people in three years, the nCHA will act as a sovereign institution empowered to fast-track housing and infrastructure development in California.

The nCHA is envisioned as an independent, constitutionally authorized, self-sustaining authority that builds, operates, and governs housing developments, transit networks, energy production systems, food security programs, and comprehensive social services. It is intended not only to solve urgent housing and development challenges in California but also to serve as a replicable proof-of-concept for the nation and the world.

PRACTICAL NECESSITIES FOR THE nCHA

1. Housing Affordability & Shortages

  • Median home prices in California exceeded $800,000 in 2024, more than twice the national average.
  • Over 181,000 individuals in California are currently unhoused, accounting for nearly 30% of the total U.S. homeless population.
  • Low-income renters face a shortage of over 980,000 affordable rental homes, according to the National Low Income Housing Coalition.

2. Broken Development Processes

  • Environmental reviews and permitting processes delay construction by 5–10 years on average.
  • Fragmented jurisdiction creates duplicated and conflicting requirements.
  • Litigation from competing interests frequently halts projects before ground is broken.

3. Lack of Integrated Infrastructure

  • Transit remains concentrated in coastal metros while inland areas are underserved.
  • The energy grid is fragile and lacks localized renewable sources.
  • Food security remains vulnerable, especially in rural and low-income areas.

4. Climate & Social Imperatives

  • California is at risk of missing both housing and climate targets.
  • Working Californians are increasingly unable to afford rent or build wealth.

THE STRUCTURE & MISSION OF THE nCHA

The New California Housing Authority (nCHA) will operate independently from existing regulatory systems, with powers to meet the state’s housing and infrastructure needs.

1. Housing Construction at Unprecedented Scale

  • Design and build millions of climate-resilient housing units.
  • Prioritize undeveloped or underutilized land.
  • Include transportation, healthcare, education, and food access in every development.

2. Regulatory Supersession & Acceleration

  • nCHA may override zoning restrictions and environmental delays.
  • Use internal review processes to replace CEQA where needed.
  • Maintain limited legal liability to avoid obstruction.

3. Expanded Eminent Domain

  • Acquire and redevelop:
    • Vacant and underutilized land
    • Dangerous or substandard housing
    • Public lands stalled in administrative limbo

INFRASTRUCTURE, ENERGY & FOOD DEVELOPMENT

A. Energy Production

  • Build solar, wind, and small modular nuclear reactors.
  • Construct tidal power facilities and sea walls.
  • Invest in cold fusion, hydrogen, and battery R&D.
  • Use energy revenue to fund UBI and social services.

B. Transportation Infrastructure

  • Develop high-speed rail and long-distance tunnels.
  • Build transit-oriented housing hubs.

C. Sustainable Food Systems

  • Implement vertical farming and synthetic protein production.
  • Guarantee low-cost, healthy food access for residents.
  • Create employment in food tech and distribution.

SOCIAL & HUMAN DEVELOPMENT MANDATE

A. Employment & Career Development

  • Every nCHA project must create short- and long-term jobs.
  • Provide career coaching, income-share employment, and job placement.

B. Sliding-Scale UBI

  • Fund UBI through energy and food surplus and income-sharing programs.
  • Allow residents to choose reduced working years or early retirement.

C. Childcare, Education & Elder Care

  • Embed childcare centers and early education programs.
  • Build elder care facilities integrated into housing.

HARM REDUCTION & UNHOUSED SERVICES

A. Safe Sheltering Campuses

  • Provide voluntary long-term shelter for the unhoused.
  • Distribute legal, synthetic substances under agreements to remain onsite.
  • Offer non-coercive paths to sobriety and employment.

B. Experimental Legal Facilities

  • Where necessary, develop private incarceration or rehab centers.
  • Ensure humane conditions, independent oversight, and reentry support.

GOVERNANCE & ACCOUNTABILITY

  • Operate with legal immunity and administrative independence.
  • Undergo mandatory 5-year reviews to maintain operation.
  • Participation in services remains voluntary and competitive.

FEDERAL & GLOBAL MODELING

The nCHA is more than a housing agency — it is an experiment in state-scale innovation. California is positioned to lead by example and provide a model for solving crises of housing, infrastructure, and climate that cities across the world now face.
